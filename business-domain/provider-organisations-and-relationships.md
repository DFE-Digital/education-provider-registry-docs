## Business Definition

Education providers do not always operate as isolated organisations.

Some are individual schools or centres. Some are operated by academy trusts. Some maintained schools share governance through a federation. Some academy arrangements involve sponsors. Some children's centres work through local group or collaboration arrangements.

This document describes the real-world organisations and relationships that sit around education providers.

The core business question is:

```text
Which organisation, trust, federation, sponsor or collaboration is connected to this provider?
```

That is different from:

```text
What kind of provider is this?
```

A provider can be a community school, academy, free school, college, nursery school or children's centre. Separately, it may be connected to an academy trust, federation, sponsor, children's-centre group or collaboration.

## Why These Relationships Matter

The education landscape is not only a list of individual schools, colleges and centres.

Some responsibilities sit with the individual provider. For example, an individual school has a name, address, inspection record, age range and local provision.

Other responsibilities sit with a wider organisation. For example, an academy trust has a board, members, trustees, funding-agreement responsibilities, financial controls and governance duties across the academies it operates.

Some relationships sit between providers. For example, maintained schools may federate under one governing body while remaining separate schools.

These relationships help answer questions such as:

- Which academies are operated by this trust?
- Is this maintained school part of a federation?
- Which schools are associated with this sponsor?
- Which children's centres work together as a group or collaboration?
- Which organisation-level identifiers apply to the trust or group rather than to an individual school?
- Which governance people act at trust, federation or group level?
- Has a trust, federation or group closed, changed or been replaced?

## Provider, Organisation And Relationship

There are three ideas to keep separate:

| Idea | Business Question | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Education provider | Which individual school, academy, college, centre or provider is being described? | Oakfield Academy. |
| Provider organisation | Which wider organisation or governance structure sits around one or more providers? | Oakfield Learning Trust. |
| Provider relationship | How is the provider connected to that organisation or to another provider? | Oakfield Academy is operated by Oakfield Learning Trust. |

Example:

```text
Provider:
Oakfield Academy

Provider kind:
Academy converter

Provider organisation:
Oakfield Learning Trust

Organisation kind:
Multi-academy trust

Relationship:
The academy is operated by the trust.
```

These facts belong together, but they are not the same fact.

## Academy Trusts

An academy trust is the legal organisation responsible for one or more academies.

GOV.UK defines an academy trust as an independent charitable company limited by guarantee with a funding agreement with the Secretary of State for Education. Academy trusts can run academies, free schools, university technical colleges and studio schools.

An academy trust is therefore both:

- The legal organisation accountable for the academies it operates.
- The wider organisation that connects those academies together.

The trust has its own governance structure. GOV.UK identifies the trust board as the accountable body for the academy trust. The trust board is made up of academy trustees, who are also company directors and charity trustees.

The Academy Trust Handbook adds the financial and control context. Academy trusts must comply with the handbook as a condition of their funding agreement. The handbook provides the framework for financial management and other controls for academy trusts as publicly funded bodies.

In business terms:

```text
The academy is the school.
The academy trust is the legal organisation accountable for that school.
```

## Multi-Academy Trusts

A multi-academy trust, or MAT, is an academy trust responsible for more than one academy.

GOV.UK describes a MAT as a single legal entity. It is a charitable company with a board of academy trustees that is accountable for all the academies in the trust. It has a master funding agreement and supplemental funding agreements for each academy.

This matters because a MAT is not a loose federation of separate academy companies. The MAT is one legal organisation. The individual academies remain separate schools or providers, but they sit within the same trust.

In business terms:

```text
A MAT is one trust.
It operates multiple academies.
The academies remain individual schools or providers.
The trust board is accountable for the trust as a whole.
```

Example:

```text
Multi-academy trust:
River Learning Trust

Academies operated by the trust:
- River Primary Academy
- River Secondary Academy
- River Studio School

Each academy is an individual provider.
The MAT is the legal organisation around them.
```

## Single-Academy Trusts

A single-academy trust, or SAT, is an academy trust responsible for one academy.

GOV.UK describes a SAT as a charitable company with a board of academy trustees that is accountable for one academy. It has a single funding agreement with the Secretary of State for Education.

In business terms:

```text
A SAT is still an academy trust.
It is still a legal organisation.
It happens to operate one academy rather than several.
```

This distinction is important. A single-academy trust is not the same thing as the academy itself. The trust is the legal organisation. The academy is the school or provider operated by that trust.

## Secure Single-Academy Trusts

A secure single-academy trust is a specialised form of single-academy trust associated with secure 16 to 19 academy provision.

In business terms, it should be treated as:

- A trust-style legal organisation.
- A specialised SAT subtype.
- Connected to secure academy provision.

The important point is that the secure trust classification describes the trust organisation, not only the school or provider.

## Free Schools, UTCs And Studio Schools

Free schools, university technical colleges and studio schools are often discussed as types of provider, but GOV.UK academy trust guidance is clear that academy trusts can operate them.

This means there are two related but different pieces of information:

| Question | Example Answer |
| --- | --- |
| What kind of provider is this? | Free school, UTC, studio school, academy converter. |
| Which organisation operates it? | A single-academy trust or multi-academy trust. |

The GOV.UK open academies dataset reinforces this landscape by publishing open academies, free schools, studio schools, UTCs and academy projects in development.

For business purposes, do not collapse provider kind into operating organisation. A free school is a kind of provider. The academy trust is the legal organisation that operates it.

## Federations

A federation is a statutory maintained-school governance arrangement.

GOV.UK federation guidance defines a federation as two or more maintained schools operating under the governance of a single governing body. The guidance applies to maintained schools and maintained nursery schools.

This is different from a multi-academy trust.

In a federation:

- The member schools remain maintained schools.
- The member schools remain separate schools.
- The federation creates one shared governing body.
- The federation does not create one new school replacing the member schools.

In business terms:

```text
A federation is shared maintained-school governance.
It is not an academy trust.
It is not a new school replacing the member schools.
```

Example:

```text
Federation:
North and South Primary Federation

Member schools:
- North Street Primary School
- South Road Primary School

The schools remain separate maintained schools.
The federation represents their shared maintained-school governance arrangement.
```

Academies do not normally join maintained-school federations. Academy grouping is handled through academy trusts.

## School Sponsors

A school sponsor is a body connected with the sponsorship of schools, especially sponsor-led academy arrangements.

In business terms, sponsorship is about support, intervention, governance strength and improvement responsibility. A sponsor may be involved in bringing a school into the academy sector or supporting a sponsored academy.

This should be kept distinct from the academy trust itself.

The practical distinction is:

| Concept | Business Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Academy trust | The legal organisation accountable for one or more academies. |
| School sponsor | A sponsoring body associated with support, sponsorship or improvement of one or more schools or academies. |

In some real-world cases the sponsor and trust may be closely related. For business explanation, it is still useful to keep the concepts separate because they answer different questions.

## Children's Centre Groups And Collaborations

Children's centres can also work through group and collaboration arrangements.

A children's centre group is best understood as an operational grouping of children's centres, often with a lead-centre arrangement.

A children's centre collaboration is best understood as a collaboration arrangement between children's centres or between children's-centre groups.

The GOV.UK sources listed above do not define these group types in the same way they define academy trusts or federations. They should therefore be treated as locally defined business concepts unless a stronger national definition is agreed.

The important business point is still clear:

```text
The children's centre remains the provider.
The group or collaboration is the arrangement around one or more children's centres.
```

## Generic Trust-Style Arrangements

There may also be generic trust-style arrangements.

This should not be confused with an academy trust. In business explanation, use "generic trust" or "legacy trust arrangement" where possible, because simply saying "trust" can mislead readers into assuming an academy trust.

This concept needs careful wording because "trust" can mean different things in different education contexts.

## Provider Relationships

A provider relationship describes the link between an individual provider and a wider organisation or arrangement.

In business terms:

```text
This school, academy, centre or provider belongs to, is governed by, is sponsored by,
or is otherwise linked to this organisation or arrangement.
```

The relationship may need its own facts, such as:

- When the provider joined the trust, federation, sponsor arrangement or collaboration.
- Whether the relationship is current.
- Whether the relationship has a special role, such as lead centre.
- Whether the relationship changed because of conversion, closure, reorganisation or trust change.

This is why the relationship should be treated as a business fact in its own right, not just as a descriptive label on the provider.

## Organisation-To-Organisation Relationships

Some relationships are between organisations rather than between a provider and an organisation.

For example, a single-academy trust may later become part of, or be succeeded by, a multi-academy trust. The individual academy may remain recognisable as a school, but the organisation around it has changed.

In business terms:

```text
Organisation-to-organisation relationships preserve history between trusts, groups and other provider organisations.
```

This is important for understanding trust changes over time, not just the current relationship for a school.

## Information About Provider Organisations

For business purposes, it may be useful to know:

- The organisation name.
- The organisation type.
- Whether the organisation is active or closed.
- Organisation-level identifiers.
- The schools, academies, centres or providers connected to it.
- The dates when providers joined or left.
- Organisation-to-organisation relationships, especially where trust structures change.
- Governance people associated with the organisation.
- Contact and address information for the organisation.

This does not mean every provider organisation has every item of information. It means the organisation is a distinct business concept, separate from the providers it links together.

## Business Rules And Boundaries

The following boundaries are important:

- A provider organisation is not the same thing as an individual school, academy, centre or provider.
- An academy trust is both a legal organisation and an accountable body for academies.
- A MAT is one legal trust entity with multiple academies.
- A SAT is one legal trust entity with one academy.
- A federation applies to maintained schools, not academies.
- A federation does not merge its member schools into one school.
- A school sponsor should not be treated as identical to the academy trust unless the business evidence says it is the same organisation in a particular case.
- Children's centre groups and collaborations need clear business definitions.
- Provider kind and provider organisation type must remain separate concepts.

## Plain-English Glossary

| Term | Plain-English Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Education provider | An individual school, academy, college, children's centre or provider. |
| Provider organisation | A wider organisation or governance structure around one or more providers. |
| Provider relationship | The relationship linking a provider to a trust, federation, sponsor, group or collaboration. |
| Academy trust | The legal organisation accountable for one or more academies. |
| MAT | One academy-trust legal entity accountable for multiple academies. |
| SAT | One academy-trust legal entity accountable for one academy. |
| Federation | Two or more maintained schools under one governing body. |
| School sponsor | A body associated with sponsoring or supporting schools, especially sponsor-led academy arrangements. |
| Children's centre group | An operational grouping of children's centres. |
| Children's centre collaboration | A collaboration arrangement involving children's centres or children's-centre groups. |
| Organisation relationship | A relationship between two provider organisations, often used to preserve trust-structure history. |

## Business Implication

Provider organisations and relationships should be recognised as important business concepts in their own right.

That means:

- Do not treat the wider organisation only as a label on a school or provider.
- Do not confuse provider kind with organisational relationship.
- Do not treat all provider organisations as academy trusts.
- Keep the provider, the wider organisation and the relationship between them distinct.
- Preserve relationship history where it explains trust changes, federation changes or children's-centre group changes.

This gives a clearer way to describe the real education landscape: individual schools and providers, the organisations around them, and the relationships between the two.
